Mapping: Systems thinking, insight, foresight, signal mapping & trend mapping.

I am a proficient systems thinker and I enjoy mapping out concepts, theories and the relationships between technologies and trends. These technologies often manifest as signals of upcoming trends. By maintaining this practice as new signals and trends arise, I hope to develop comprehensive maps of innovation and human behaviour.

The above maps are on Data as Material, "A Culture of Moments" (Alexander Manu, Transforming Organizations for the Subscription Economy: Starting from Scratch Routledge, 2017) and The Opt-In future.

Data as Material is the idea of data creation and collection serving as the basis of design and business activity today. Users produce and consume data which leads to the production and consumption of more data. This has reshaped business model design and creates new opprtunities for networks, services and experience optimization.

Data as Material produces a culture of moments. DaM reinvented conventional value propositions by putting the value delivery at the center of the experience. These new forms of value delivery are usually seamless and quick and they create ecosystems like the sharing and streaming economies and XaaS.

DaM and A Culture of Moments in tandem with the automation and augmentation of tasks is shaping the Opt-In Future , a future wherein humanity is freed from mundane tasks and able to focus on human pursuits. Automation and augmentation in the home and workplace already save us time and the Opt-In Future is all about who we will become during the time saved.

User-centered design: User experience design, user interface design, user testing, problem solving, design lead

I am the designer at Xesto, a University of Toronto accelerated computer vision startup. I designed the Xesto app and brand identity for the company, both of which launched in November 2020. The app harnesses the power of the FaceID camera in iPhones X and above to help users scan and size their feet for online shoe shopping.

Constraint: The Xesto app relies on the FaceID camera in iPhones X and up for TrueDepth scanning. Therefore, a constraint baked into the app is that the scanning process takes place with the phone screen facing away from the user.

We started by testing 3 different scanning user interfaces with users who had never been exposed to the products before. We got strong feedback that the "target" method of lining the camera up with an image of a foot was far too difficult, though multiple users had suggested it at an earlier stage. We realized that while our first UI was too open ended, the target method was too restrictive.

Opportunity: We asked "How can the app hold the user's hand throughout the scanning process?" and paid attention to how we would explain the scanning process when we intervened in unsuccessful user testing. This led us to the idea of a guided UI wherein Xesto checks in with the user throughout the scanning process.

Result: With the guided UI, approximately 80% of users took successful scans for the first time during our last round of testing. The key to this UI was pauses between each photograph that require the user's manual approval. "Does your photo look like this?" and a visual cue reduced error by a large margin.

The Primacy of the How: a design framework that puts the value delivery at the center of the value proposition.

Alexander Manu Transforming Organizations for the Subscription Economy: Starting from Scratch Routledge, 2017.

The most important concept I have been thinking about lately is The Primacy of the How from Alexander Manu's Transforming Organizations. The Primacy of the How suggests that the how has usurped the what at the center of the value proposition. This is to say that how value is delivered is more important than the creation of value itself. This assertion is predicated on the fact that the what of behaviour platforms today usually corresponds to a number of latent human behaviours. We use Facebook because we are social, networked beings, we send Snapchats because we can and we use Uber because we need to get from point a to point b.

The Primacy of the How as an exercise involves asking the question: what is the question that X is the answer to? For example: what is the question that Uber is the answer to? A: How do I get from point a to point b without having to drive myself? How do I summon a form of transportation in the most seamless manner possible? How do I monetize my vehicle in my free time? These questions are answered by a platform that gets people where they are going quickly and seamlessly, being driven by another individual who has a car in the moment that you don't. If the designers of Uber had simply asked what (i.e. what is a problem we can solve?), they would have designed a bicycle. Instead they asked how, the same way the founders of Airbnb did, and invented an entirely sharing new economy based on value delivery.

The Primacy of the How is also an incredibly powerful framework for idea generation. Think deeply about what users want and evaluate how they are getting it. My wifi is provided by Bell through fiberoptic cables but Elon Musk's Starlink seems like a better way. I ordered my first big grocery delivery the other day due to lockdown restrictions and was amazed by the speed of the service. My girlfriend's aunt just discovered DoorDash and was amazed by how quickly it satisfied her craving for Baskin and Robbin's ice cream. That's what the primacy of the how is all about.
